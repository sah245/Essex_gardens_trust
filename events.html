Dr David Marsh on "The Great Geranium Robbery"

In the late 18th century, Britain’s expanding empire brought many new and exotic plants from around the world into commercial production and private gardens through specialist nurserymen like Daniel Grimwood of Kensington. In 1795, rare plants, including newly introduced geraniums and ericas from South Africa, were stolen from his nursery. A suspect was arrested but denied guilt, and the case went to the Old Bailey, where Grimwood faced the accused plant collector. Beyond the trial, the story highlights the rapid expansion of plant varieties in Britain and how nurseries operated.

Dr Alison Moller on “Pioneering English Plantswomen”

Garden design remained largely male-dominated until the late 19th century, with women discouraged from leading large projects. However, three women earned respect for their contributions. In the 17th century, Mary, Duchess of Beaufort and Queen Mary II focused on studying, propagating, and documenting new plants, gaining recognition from male experts. By the 19th century, Maria Jacson went further, studying botany and entering garden design, helping pave the way for other women despite significant barriers.
